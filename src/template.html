The bottom line

AI coding tools produce real gains at the task level, but almost none of it currently reaches delivery speed, cost, or revenue, because the binding constraint is verification, not code authoring. INFERRED Three findings from the literature support this, and one operational change follows.

  1. Task gains are real but conditional. MEASURED Controlled experiments show 14–56% speedups on well-scoped, checkable work. The effect reverses for experienced engineers on familiar, judgment-heavy code. One rigorous trial measured them 19% slower while they believed they were 20% faster.
  2. The gains stall at review. MEASURED Telemetry across 10,000+ developers: 98% more pull requests merged, 91% longer review times, and no movement in delivery metrics (lead time, deployment frequency). Roughly nine in ten of ~6,000 surveyed firms report no productivity impact at all. More code out of the editor does not mean more product out the door.
  3. Agentic economics are governed by two curves. MEASURED Session costs are heavy-tailed by construction (median $0.61, mean $9.70, top-1% $178), and the expensive tail is mostly failed retries. INFERRED Capping any task at roughly its 90th-percentile budget should remove most spend at almost no quality loss. Meanwhile total delivery improvement is capped by how much of the pipeline the tool spans: accelerate authoring alone and the ceiling is ~1.33×, no matter how good the model gets.

Recommendation: instrument cost per merged change and session-cost percentiles before scaling anything; enforce per-task budget caps in tooling; deploy autonomous loops only where an automated correctness check (an "oracle" — tests, compilers, typed specs) is cheap and the loop spans the actual bottleneck. Everything else stays assist-mode or human. Reassess when metered billing data matures after the September 2026 promotional cliff.

Reading the arc: four eras in forty-two months

Era I · 2023 — The task-level RCT wave

The founding studies were randomized controlled trials (RCTs) on well-scoped tasks, and they found large effects: Copilot users finished a scripting task 56% faster (Peng et al.), ChatGPT users wrote professional documents 40% faster at higher quality (Noy & Zhang, Science), and a staggered rollout across 5,172 call-center agents lifted resolutions per hour ~14–15% (Brynjolfsson, Li & Raymond, later published in the QJE). A recurring second finding: gains concentrated among novices and weaker performers — "skill compression."

Era II · 2023–24 — Heterogeneity and the jagged frontier

The BCG consultant experiment (Dell'Acqua et al.) complicated the picture: inside the model's capability frontier, consultants were faster and better; outside it, they were 19 points more likely to be wrong, because they trusted fluent output on tasks the model couldn't do. The Kenya entrepreneur RCT (Otis et al.) found outright divergence — AI advice helped strong operators and hurt weak ones — cutting against the skill-compression story. Effect direction, it emerged, is conditional on task structure and user judgment, not a property of the technology.

Era III · 2025 — The ecological-validity correction

Three shocks landed in one year. METR's RCT put experienced open-source developers on their own repositories and found they were 19% slower with AI. The same developers believed they were 20% faster. No cleaner demonstration exists that self-report is not measurement. Humlum & Vestergaard linked 25,000 Danish workers to administrative payroll records and found ~2.8% time savings, no detectable effect on earnings or hours, and only 3–7% of gains passing through to pay. And the field absorbed a credibility shock: MIT disavowed the celebrated Toner-Rodgers materials-science paper — praised by Acemoglu and Autor, cited in policy speeches — stating it had no confidence in the data's provenance, reliability or validity, and requesting the paper's withdrawal. A pattern worth sitting with: the studies with the largest effects were the least real, and the most real settings showed the smallest effects.

Era IV · 2025–26 — The micro–macro paradox, and measurement breaking down

By 2026 the contradiction is formalized. Firm-level surveys (Yotzov, Bloom et al., ~6,000 executives across four countries) find 69% adoption but roughly nine in ten firms reporting no productivity or employment impact yet — a Solow paradox restated. Simultaneously, revised BLS aggregates show US productivity growth near 2.7% in 2025, and both Brynjolfsson and a previously skeptical Furman now read an AI signal in the macro data via the J-curve. Meanwhile the gold-standard instrument broke: METR's follow-up RCT collapsed because developers refused to work in the no-AI control arm — adoption is now so deep that clean counterfactuals may no longer be constructible. The open question has inverted from "does AI raise productivity?" to "can we still measure the counterfactual at all?"

Cross-cutting patterns

Effect size falls as realism rises. INFERRED Order the studies by ecological validity (lab task, then field experiment, then linked administrative data, then firm P&L) and the headline effects shrink monotonically from ~56% to ~0%. No other regularity in this graph is as robust.

Task structure predicts gains. MEASURED Stanford's 2026 Index synthesis finds gains track how verifiable the work is: strong for support, coding, and marketing output; weak-to-negative for unstructured judgment work. This reconciles most apparent contradictions without invoking bad faith on either side.

Perception is systematically inflated. MEASURED METR's 39-point perception gap, the CFO "perceived > measured" paradox, and the EEG self-report gaps all point the same direction: subjective productivity reports overstate measured effects, which should discount every survey-based node in the graph.

Institutional gravity. INFERRED A remarkably small network — Brynjolfsson (Stanford), the MIT/NBER economics cluster, Microsoft Research — sits upstream of most positive findings, while the strongest null results come from linked-registry Scandinavian data and central-bank survey infrastructure. Neither camp is wrong; they are measuring different layers of the same system at different lags.

Appendix: the heavy tail of agentic cost

The cost of fully agentic sessions is not merely high on average. It is concentrated in a heavy tail. MEASURED The cleanest measurement is TraceLab's characterization of real coding-agent workloads: a session costs $9.70 on average but only $0.61 at the median, with a p99 of $178 — a mean/median ratio of ~16× and a p99/median ratio of ~292×. Independent SWE-bench measurements find token usage varies up to 30× across runs of the same task, that models predict their own consumption only weakly (Pearson r ≤ 0.39), and — critically — that accuracy peaks at intermediate cost: the expensive tail is disproportionately thrashing, not thoroughness.

The tail is generated multiplicatively. Every API call is stateless, so accumulated context is replayed on each step — prefix tokens make up 59.5% of total cost despite being billed at ~one-tenth the fresh-input rate, and input outnumbers output by 20–150× in agentic workloads. Per-step cost therefore grows with loop position, and total session cost grows superlinearly in loop length, which is itself stochastic. The known mitigations each attack one term of that product: prompt caching cuts the unit price of replayed context (volume still dominates spend), context pruning and compaction shrink what gets replayed, prompt compression lowers the baseline, and checkpointed agent architectures avoid full-history replay.INFERRED None of them touch loop count, which is why budget caps stay necessary even in a well-engineered stack:

Field incidents are this distribution integrated over an organization: Uber exhausted its annual AI-coding budget in four months and imposed a $1,500/month per-engineer cap; one 100-agent deployment hit $1.3M in a single month, ~70% of it attributable to one configuration flag; and GitHub's June 2026 metering transferred the tail from provider to customer, surfacing 10–50× bill shocks among agentic power users. The managerial implication follows directly from the accuracy-vs-cost curve: because marginal tail tokens are anticorrelated with success, aggressive per-task budget caps with human escalation truncate the majority of spend at near-zero expected quality loss. Appendix B: the bottleneck thesis — why loops might still be the unlock

The strongest case for agentic loops is simple to state: generation was never the constraint, and loops are the first tool shaped like the thing that is. INFERRED The delivery-telemetry evidence locates it precisely. Faros AI's instrumented study of 10,000+ developers found high-AI-adoption teams completed 21% more tasks and merged 98% more pull requests — while PR review time rose 91%, PR size grew 154%, and org-level DORA metrics stayed flat. DORA 2025 finds the same shape from the survey side: higher AI adoption raises throughput and instability together, with time saved in creation re-allocated to auditing — the "verification tax." Atlassian's 2026 workforce research independently named it the AI efficiency paradox: output backs up at the points requiring human judgment. Task-level gains are real; they queue at the review-verify-integrate stages and die there. This is also the single best mechanistic explanation for the micro–macro paradox in the main graph.

The unlock evidence is early but loop-shaped. MEASURED SWE-Review shows that a generate-review-revise loop lifts SWE-bench Verified resolve rates from 27.5% to 56.9% (and 50.9% to 68.8% on a stronger base) — closed-loop review beats one-shot generation decisively, attacking exactly the stage where Faros shows gains queuing. Tests-in-the-loop converts generation from plausible to verified: the agent isn't "done" until the oracle says so, which is the practitioner consensus (Willison, Osmani) and the mechanism behind CI automation results like test cycles falling 6h→1.5h with defect detection rising 70%→92%. Observational work on agent adoption (Kumar et al.) finds the same lever from the context side: decomposition plus injected project knowledge is what separates successful delegation from failed delegation. And the classic DORA chain (2023 report) — teams with fast code review show ~50% better delivery performance, and delivery performance predicts organizational performance — is the established transmission path from lead-time compression to revenue: shorter cycles mean more shipped experiments per quarter, faster learning, and earlier revenue realization per feature.

Assembled as a falsifiable thesis: assistants raise s on a small f; loops raise f — and only raising f changes the delivery ceiling. INFERRED The reconciliation with this page's null results is then a frontier story, not a contradiction: firms measure nothing because current deployments accelerate the non-binding stage, and the tail appendix's cost pathology is the price of running loops without cheap oracles. The conditions for the flip are checkable in advance: (1) the loop spans the binding constraint (review/verification, not authoring); (2) verification oracles are mechanical and cheap, so iterations converge instead of thrash; (3) per-task budget caps truncate the tail; (4) batch sizes stay reviewable so instability doesn't eat the throughput. Where all four hold, task gains should transmit to lead time within quarters, and lead time to cost and revenue through the DORA channel. Where they don't, expect Faros curves: more PRs, longer queues, flat P&L. The bet, priced honestly, is not "AI writes code" — it's "verification becomes software."

From evidence to practice: a decision framework

The literature above compresses into an operating rule: match the mode to the oracle. The cost of a loop is governed by how many iterations it takes to converge, and iterations are governed by how cheaply correctness can be checked. Where verification is mechanical, loops converge and the economics work; where verification is human judgment, loops thrash into the tail and assist-mode adds review burden without removing the constraint.

A rough taxonomy of oracles, cheapest first, sets the deployment order.INFERRED Level 1, deterministic: compilers, type systems, linters, schema validators; near-zero marginal cost, safe to loop against thousands of times. Level 2, behavioral: unit and integration suites, contract tests, static analysis; cheap when fast and reliable, but a flaky suite poisons a loop faster than no suite at all. Level 3, subjective: human review, architectural judgment, taste; expensive, slow, and the level where loops thrash. Expected agent ROI falls roughly an order of magnitude per level. Design work down this hierarchy, not up it.

Instrument before you scale. Seat-level spend is the wrong denominator; the unit economics that decide the question are cost per merged change, session-cost percentiles (p50/p95/p99 — the tail, not the mean, is the budget), retry and loop-iteration counts, repeat-read ratio (context replay waste), PR size and review-queue depth (the constraint's vital signs), and DORA lead time with change-fail rate (whether anything reaches delivery). An organization that cannot see its session-cost histogram cannot distinguish its frontier experiments from its waste — and will misclassify both.

Re-engagement preconditions, restated as a checklist: (1) the loop spans the currently binding constraint, not the convenient stage; (2) the verification oracle is mechanical and cheap; (3) per-task budget caps with human escalation are enforced by tooling, not discipline; (4) batch sizes stay reviewable so throughput gains don't convert to instability. Four yes answers: scale the loop. Any no: the Faros curve is the expected outcome.

Where this leaves the ledger

Read together, the three parts of this page make one claim, with three governors on it. INFERRED Task-level gains are real and conditional on task structure (the graph). Whether they reach delivery, cost, or revenue is governed by f — the fraction of lead time the loop actually spans (Appendix B). Whether spanning it is affordable is governed by oracle cost — cheap mechanical verification truncates the tail that otherwise carries most of the spend (Appendix A). Assistants without loops hit the Amdahl ceiling. Loops without oracles hit the cost tail. Loops with oracles across the binding constraint are the only configuration with a live path to the firm-level numbers that remain, as of mid-2026, missing. Checkable waypoints: the post-promotional Copilot billing cycles from September 2026 (does agentic usage survive honest pricing?), and whether any registry-linked study finds firm-level revenue or margin effects before 2030.
